Shoot it yourself:
the phone photo guide

You don’t always have a photographer on call. Here’s how to take clean, bright, listing-ready photos with the phone already in your pocket — the same fundamentals our team relies on before we ever touch an image.

A 10-minute read · keep it on your phone for your next walkthrough
01 · Before you shoot

Win the photo in the room, not the edit

The best editing can’t save a cluttered, dim room. Five minutes of prep beats an hour of retouching.

  • Clear the surfaces. Counters, tables, nightstands, the top of the fridge. Buyers read clutter as “small.”
  • Depersonalise. Family photos, fridge magnets, toiletries, pet bowls, bins — out of frame.
  • Open it up. Blinds up, curtains back, every light on, ceiling fans off (blades blur).
  • Clean the glass. Windows and mirrors show every smudge at high resolution.
  • Wipe your lens. The single most common cause of soft, hazy phone photos.
02 · Light

Shoot with the light, never against it

Light is 80% of a good real estate photo. Aim for soft and even, not bright and harsh.

  • Best window: mid-morning or late afternoon on a bright-overcast day. Harsh noon sun throws hot spots and black shadows.
  • Turn every light on — lamps, overheads, under-cabinet. Warm pools of light make a home feel lived-in.
  • Don’t point at the window. Shoot so the main window is to your side; the room lights evenly instead of going dark against the glare.
  • Kill the flash. On-phone flash flattens the room and blows out the nearest wall.
Exteriors: shoot the front of the home when the sun is behind you, lighting the façade — usually morning for east-facing, afternoon for west-facing. A blue sky and green lawn do a lot of the work. (We can also turn a flat daytime exterior into a twilight hero shot.)
03 · Height & angle

Chest height, straight lines, into a corner

  • Hold the phone at chest height (about 4–4.5 ft) and keep it level. Shooting from the eye or the hip distorts the room.
  • Keep verticals vertical. Door frames and wall edges should run straight up and down, not lean. Turn on your camera grid and line them up.
  • Shoot into a corner, not flat at a wall. A two-wall angle shows depth and makes rooms feel larger.
  • Back into the doorway to capture the whole room. Get as much floor and ceiling context as you can.
  • Landscape (horizontal) for rooms; portrait only for tall details.
04 · Phone settings

Four settings that do the heavy lifting

  • Grid on (Settings → Camera). Your level and your framing guide.
  • HDR on / auto. Balances a bright window against a darker room so both are exposed.
  • Tap to focus, then set exposure. Tap the mid-tone of the room; slide exposure down until the window stops glowing.
  • Highest resolution, and hold still. Brace against a wall or door frame, or use a cheap tripod. Blur is unfixable.
05 · A light hand in editing

Enhance, never fake

A gentle lift is fine. Straighten, nudge brightness and shadows, and warm it slightly. Don’t over-saturate, over-widen, or edit out permanent features — buyers notice, and it’s a disclosure risk.

Do

  • Straighten and level
  • Lift shadows a touch
  • Even, natural white balance
  • Crop out clutter at the edges

Don’t

  • Crank saturation or “vivid” filters
  • Stretch the lens ultra-wide
  • Remove or add rooms/features
  • Sky-swap into a season you’re not in
06 · The shot list

Capture these, in this order

Work front to back so nothing gets missed.

ExteriorStraight-on façade, then a ¾ angle. Backyard and any view.
Living roomFrom the doorway into the corner; a second angle back toward the entry.
KitchenWide from the entry, then a counter/island detail. Include appliances.
DiningShow it set or clear, with the light fixture in frame.
Primary bedroomBed centred from the doorway; note the closet and ensuite.
Other bedroomsOne clean wide shot each.
BathroomsAngle to avoid catching yourself in the mirror.
Details & amenitiesFireplace, view, balcony, laundry, garage, building amenities.
